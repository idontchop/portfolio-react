Early work...

I've heard programmers are often asked how old they were when they first started writing code. Answer: 13. And when did I complete and release my first piece of software? I was 15. It was a BBS door game written in Turbo C++ using the OpenDoors Library. I even made a couple hundred bucks on it.

Alas, the World Wide Web interrupted my plans for the SciFi war game sequel.

After high school I went to work for an ISP, shortly before cable internet. Using the provider's T1 line, I installed a Debian server and learned how to host web sites (and mail, dns, mysql, etc). A year or so later, I had a few servers colocated with that ISP's ISP, hee hee.

In another few years, after struggling through the dot-com crash, I sold my customer base and servers (then up to about 40) to a larger hosting company in Atlanta, helped them through the transition and then swore off the IT industry for the gambling industry. Maybe not the best decision... But I had done well for a high school graduate who started with a book on C++.

From then to now, I occasionally rediscovered coding for certain projects, such as a poker hand range equity calculator (github), and a python script to pull the crypto-coin prices from exchanges to check for trades.

And Now...

I recently graduated from Western Governor's University with a BSIT - Software Programmers degree. Just one of those things as a mid-30s adult you feel you need to do to be taken seriously. Honestly, I didn't learn much. Even the late 90s C++ programming books I studied talked of clean coding and design patterns. It's not a new idea. Sure, it's been refined, with frameworks like Spring and paradigms like functional programming. They sure take the tediousness out of the work. Not the testing and bug-hunting though. I do more now than I remember doing before, maybe because I now understand the importance of it... and there will always be bugs.

My capstone was a Spring REST backend for my Java Poker Tools app.

I'm most comfortable in Java 8, after tearing myself away from the for (;;) loops of C++.

Spring, Hibernate, and other libraries commonly used with Spring (AOP, Spring Security), are of high interest to me and is where I'm focused.

React has made Javascript and web-based UI/UX a pleasure, and I have aspirations of my first dollar made from my own app since I was a teenager... unless someone hires me first.
